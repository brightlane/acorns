The Upgrade Timeline — When to Switch

$0–$500: Acorns only (Wealthfront minimum not met). Use Round-Ups aggressively.

$500–$14,400: Wealthfront's 0.25% fee is lower in absolute dollars, but Acorns' Round-Ups may generate more returns than the fee savings. This is the judgment call zone.

$14,400+: Acorns becomes cheaper in absolute fee terms. Keep Acorns for Round-Ups, consider adding Wealthfront for its tax optimization features.

$50,000+: Wealthfront's tax-loss harvesting likely saves more than its extra fee. Consider Wealthfront as primary portfolio, keep Acorns as a Round-Up feeder.

$100,000+: Wealthfront's direct indexing becomes available — this is genuinely better than ETFs for tax efficiency at this scale.

💡 The Smart Path: Use Acorns to build the habit and accumulate your first $5,000–$15,000. Don't overthink the fee at small balances — the behavioral value of automation outweighs it. When you hit $15,000+, open a Wealthfront account and run both. You get Acorns' Round-Ups AND Wealthfront's tax efficiency.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is the minimum to open a Wealthfront account?
Wealthfront requires a $500 minimum to start investing. Acorns requires just $5. For investors just starting out, Acorns is accessible at any income level.
Does Wealthfront have tax-loss harvesting?
Yes — Wealthfront offers automated daily tax-loss harvesting on all taxable accounts above $500. It also offers direct indexing (owning individual S&P 500 stocks instead of the fund) for accounts above $100,000.
How much does Wealthfront charge?
Wealthfront charges 0.25% annually. On a $10,000 account that's $25/year vs Acorns' $36/year. On a $25,000 account it's $62.50 vs Acorns' $36. The crossover is around $14,400.
Should I close my Acorns account when I move to Wealthfront?
Not necessarily. Many investors keep Acorns running for Round-Ups (Wealthfront doesn't have this feature) and use Wealthfront for their larger primary portfolio. The $36/year Acorns fee is trivial once you have a $50,000 Wealthfront account.
